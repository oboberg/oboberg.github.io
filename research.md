My main research focuses on the evolution of stellar populations in Milky Way open and globular clusters. These clusters provide the laboratories necessary to constrain our current models of stellar formation, chemical evolution, and dynamics in our own galaxy.

### Globular Clusters
My thesis works focuses on a pair of Globular clusters, M53 (NGC 5024) and NGC 5053, which have recently been found to have large tidal tails, and possibly, a bridge like structure between the clusters. These morphological features suggest that these clusters have not only experienced strong interactions with the Galaxy, but also possibly with each other. For this research, I am collecting new, high quality photometric and spectroscopic data, to pair with N-body simulations in order create a more complete picture of how these features, and the clusters themselves, formed and are evolving. This work will provide a more dynamically accurate description of cluster formation and evolution in the Milky Way.

### Open Clusters
NGC 6791 is a very peculiar open cluster in the Milky May. The mass and age of this cluster are characteristic of an object somewhere between an open cluster and a globular cluster. Due to these physical characteristics, studies have attempted to determine if NGC 6791 shows the same chemical signatures found in globular clusters. My research on this cluster has used spectra available from the Sloan Extension for Galactic Understanding and Exploration (SEGUE) survey to search for CN and CH abundance variations among cluster members, indicative of multiple populations.
