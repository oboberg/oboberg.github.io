NGC 5053 and M 53

 

A brief intro to globular clusters

Globular clusters provide a crucial laboratory for us to test our theories of stellar formation and evolution. Globular clusters are made up of hundreds of thousands of stars that are gravitationally bound in spherical shape, hence the name globular. The stars in globular cluster are all at approximately the same distance from the sun, have the same chemical makeup, and were born at the same time, making them simple stellar populations. By combining these shared characteristics with our knowledge of the internal physics of stars, we are able to measure things like the age, chemical content, and distance to globular clusters. Because globular cluster are some of the oldest objects in the Milky Way, these measurements provide with a fossil record of what our Galaxy was liked when they formed and how it might evolve in the future.

As the amount and quality of data available on globular clusters increased, it became evident that they are not as simple as we once thought. Now nearly all globular clusters have been shown to have at least chemically distinct populations that are thought to have formed and different times. Some clusters, such as M54, are even the remnants of previously accreted dwarf galaxies. Understanding how these multiple generations of stars form in a single cluster is extremely important to having a complete picture of how clusters form and how they contribute to the Milky Way.

NGC 5024 and NGC 5053

My thesis works focuses on a pair of unique globular clusters named M53 (NGC 5024) and NGC 5053. These clusters are located in a relatively isolated and low density area of the Milky Way, yet are extremely close together. They are each shown in the Digital Sky Survey (DSS) image shown at the top of the page. NGC 5053 is the loose cluster in the bottom left hand corner and M53 is the highly concentrated cluster in the upper left hand corner. They are only spearated by ~300 pc in 3D coordinates. Previous imaging surveys found that these cluster have large tidal tails, and possibly, a bridge like structure between the clusters. These morphological features suggest that these clusters have not only experienced strong interactions with the Galaxy, but also possibly with each other, which has not been seen in other globular cluster system.

The aim of my research has been to collect a complete observational dataset of these clusters covering both wide-field imaging to map their morphologies, and medium resolution spectroscopy to measure abundances and classify multiple populations. Ultimately, these results of these observational projects will be paired with N-Body simulations of clusters in similar Galactic environments to get a dynamically complete picture of their evolution.

Observations

I have collected the data for my thesis project using the Wisconsin-Indiana-Yale-NOAO (WIYN) 3.5-meter and 0.9-meter telescopes at the Kitt Peak National Observatory near Tucson Arizona. On the WIYN 3.5-meter telescope, I have used the partial One Degree Imager (ODI) to collect wide field photometric data around the two clusters listed above. I also used the MOSIAC1.1 imager and the Half Degree Imager (HDI) on the 0.9 meter telescope to collect additional photometry of the clusters. I am currently in the processing of analyzing the wide-field photometric data.

I used the Hydra multi-object spectrograph on the 3.5 meter telescope to collect medium resolution spectra (R~18,000) of red giant branch stars in each cluster. These data were collected to determine if the clusters shown the chemical signatures of multiple populations, and if so, what are there characteristics. We were also able to use the radial velocities measured in M53 to measure the amount of internal rotation in the cluster.

The abundance results for NGC 5053 and M53 have already been published.

Simulations

In the future I will be using my observational data to help constrain N-Body simulations of these clusters to better understand their formation and evolution as they orbit the Galaxy. These simulations will be done with the NBODY6 code running on Indiana University’s Big Red II supercomputer. For these simulations I will be working with Dr. Enrico Vesperini, who is also at IU.
